The k-Weisfeiler–Lehman Test

A hierarchy of increasingly powerful graph distinguishing algorithms. Each level k operates on ordered k-tuples of nodes, enabling finer structural discrimination than the classical 1-WL colour refinement.

What is k-WL?

The Weisfeiler–Lehman (WL) graph isomorphism test is a classic algorithm that iteratively refines colour labels assigned to nodes (1-WL) or k-tuples of nodes (k-WL). Two graphs are considered indistinguishable by k-WL if their final colour histograms match.

The k-WL hierarchy is strictly ordered: k-WL can distinguish every pair of graphs that (k−1)-WL can, plus more. However, no finite k makes WL a complete isomorphism test — there always exist pairs of non-isomorphic graphs that fool k-WL for any fixed k.

Algorithm Steps

1
Enumerate k-tuples

Generate all ordered k-tuples of nodes: nk total. Each tuple represents a structural context within the graph.

2
Assign initial colours

Each tuple receives a colour encoding its internal adjacency pattern, equality pattern (repeated nodes), and degree sequence.

3
Iterative refinement

For each tuple, collect the multiset of colours of all tuples reachable by swapping one position to a neighbour. Hash (old colour, multiset) → new colour.

4
Convergence check

Repeat until no colour changes occur. The stable colour histogram is the k-WL certificate of the graph.

5
Compare certificates

Two graphs are k-WL indistinguishable if and only if their certificates match. Matching certificates does not guarantee isomorphism — only a mismatch is conclusive.
